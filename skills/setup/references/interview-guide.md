# Work-history interview guide

The interview is the highest-leverage conversation in the plugin. Done well, it captures the detail behind the resume — the wins, the metrics, the decisions, the dynamics — that downstream skills lift into tailored resumes, cover letters, and interview prep.

## Before you start

Read the candidate's resume first. Note:

- Each role: title, company, dates, location.
- What's already stated: accomplishments, metrics, team size.
- What's vague or missing: no numbers, unclear scope, generic verbs ("led", "managed", "worked on").

Use this to ask sharper questions. Don't make the user re-narrate what's already explicit.

## Interview flow per role

Work through roles **most recent first**. Per role, cover seven dimensions. Ask ONE dimension at a time and wait for the answer before moving to the next.

### 1. Company context

- What did the company do? What was the product?
- Company stage when you joined — startup, growth, scale-up, enterprise?
- Approximate size — employees, revenue, users, customers?
- Market context — who were the competitors? What was happening in the space?

### 2. Your mandate

- What were you hired to do? What problem were you solving?
- What was the state of things when you arrived — team, processes, metrics, tech?
- Who did you report to? What was the org structure around you?
- How did the role evolve over your tenure? Did the scope grow or change?

### 3. Team & leadership

- How many direct / indirect reports?
- Did you build the team, inherit it, or both?
- Key hires you made and why they mattered.
- How did you develop the people on your team? Promotions, transitions, growth.
- Cross-functional relationships — which teams, what dynamics?

If the user was an IC, scope this down: "Who did you collaborate with day-to-day? Who depended on your work?"

### 4. Accomplishments & impact

For each major accomplishment, drill into:

- **What** — what specifically did you do?
- **Why** — what was the business problem or opportunity?
- **How** — what was your approach? What decisions did you make? What were the alternatives?
- **Result** — what happened? Push for specific metrics:
  - Revenue / cost impact (dollar amounts, percentages, growth rates).
  - User / customer impact (acquisition, retention, NPS, activation).
  - Efficiency (cost savings, time savings, latency, throughput).
  - Team (hiring velocity, retention, promotions, productivity).
  - Market (market share, competitive wins, new segments, geo expansion).

Aim for 2–3 strong accomplishments per recent role, fewer per older roles.

### 5. Technical & strategic depth

- What tools, platforms, technologies did you use? (Resume already lists some — confirm currency and depth.)
- What frameworks or methodologies did you introduce or change?
- What strategic decisions did you drive? What were the alternatives, and why did you pick what you picked?
- What did you build from scratch vs. what did you inherit and optimize?

### 6. Challenges & failures

- What was the hardest thing about this role?
- What didn't work? What did you learn?
- Any pivots or major course corrections?
- How did you handle organizational or political challenges?

Frame this as "what was hard," not "what was wrong" — keeps the user from going defensive or trashing former colleagues. Capture the dynamic, not the gossip.

### 7. Transition

- Why did you leave (or why are you leaving)?
- What would you say was your overall legacy in this role?
- What would you do differently if you ran it again?

## Cross-role questions (after all roles are covered)

Once all roles are done, ask these to capture patterns the user might not see themselves:

1. **Salary floor.** "Below what number is the next role not worth the conversation?"
2. **Heartbeat role.** "What's the role you'd take in a heartbeat if it appeared tomorrow?"
3. **3-year narrative.** "What story do you want to be telling in 3 years?"
4. **Honest gaps.** "What are you NOT good at, that you've learned to live with?"
5. **Superpower.** "What would you say is the thing you do better than most? Not the thing you've done most — the thing you do BETTER."

For #5, push back if the answer is generic ("execution," "communication"). Ask: "Better than what — better than how most people would do it? What's the specific thing about the way you do it?"

## Interview technique

### Push for specifics

When the answer is vague, name the gap:

- "Growth — do you remember the actual numbers? Even roughly. 'Roughly 3x' is better than 'a lot'."
- "When you say 'large team' — how many people exactly?"
- "What does 'improved conversion' mean in numbers? 12% to 18%? 4% lift?"
- "You said it was hard — hard how? The people, the tech, the leadership, the customer? What did you do about it?"

### Find the story, not the line item

- "What are you most proud of from that role?"
- "If you had to explain what you did there in 30 seconds to a CEO, what would you say?"
- "What's the headline a journalist would write about your impact there?"

### Connect the dots between roles

Listen for patterns. After role 2 or 3, you'll notice things:

- Always scaling teams from 5 → 20.
- Always inherited a broken process and rebuilt it.
- Always greenfield, never maintenance.
- Always paired with a strong VP / weak CEO / specific founder type.

Surface these toward the end of the interview, not in the middle (avoids steering the answers): "I'm noticing you've taken on greenfield problems three roles in a row — is that a pattern you've chosen, or a coincidence?"

### Be efficient with old / less relevant roles

- 10+ year old roles: 5 minutes max. Capture the headline and move on.
- Roles the candidate clearly wants to move past: don't dwell. Honor it.
- Internships / pre-career roles: usually skip unless they're load-bearing for the narrative.

### When to stop probing

- After 2 follow-ups on the same question.
- If the user signals frustration (short answers, "let's move on", "I'm not sure why this matters").
- If the question is genuinely outside their scope (asking an IC about P&L, asking a junior about hiring).

The goal is honest detail, not a confession.

## Probing prompts cheat sheet

| Shallow answer | Probe |
| --- | --- |
| "I led the migration." | "Led how — who was on the team, who decided, what would have gone differently without you?" |
| "It went well." | "What changed because of it? A number, even rough. Latency? Conversion? Headcount? Revenue?" |
| "I worked on X." | "Worked on, or owned? If you owned it, what did the before-and-after look like?" |
| "I improved performance." | "By what percentage, on which metric, measured how?" |
| "I mentored people." | "How many, over what period, what's one of them doing now?" |
| "Things were hard." | "Hard how — was it the team, the tech, the leadership, the customer? What did you do about it?" |
| "Good culture." | "Good how — autonomy? Transparency? Pace? Comp? What would you keep, what would you fix?" |
| "Bad culture." | "Bad how, specifically — political? Toxic? Slow? Unclear? Don't name names; describe the pattern." |
| "I learned a lot." | "What's a thing you learned that surprised you — that you'd tell a friend going into the same role?" |
| "I want to grow." | "Grow into what — IC depth, breadth, management, ownership of a P&L, founder track, advisor track?" |
| "Salary was fine." | "Fine for you at the time, or fine for the market? Did you negotiate? Would you negotiate harder now?" |
| "My superpower is communication." | "Better than what — better than how most people would do it? What's the specific thing about the way you do it?" |

## After the interview — synthesis

Once the interview is done, write to `profile.md` per `interview-schema.md`. Capture:

- **Raw quotes and specific examples** — these are goldmines for bullet points later. Save the user's actual phrasing in the Per-role detail section.
- **Metrics even if approximate** — "roughly 3x" beats nothing.
- **The candidate's own language** for describing their work — for downstream skills to mirror.
- **Cross-role themes and patterns** — your observation, surfaced for the user to confirm.
- **The candidate's strengths as demonstrated by evidence** — not self-assessment. "The user mentored 8 engineers over 3 years; 3 were promoted" is stronger than "the user is a great mentor."

The Stories bank pulls the strongest accomplishments into a reusable form for `interview-prep` and `tailor-resume`. Each story should map to one or more behavioral question categories (leadership, failure, ambiguity, cross-functional, scale, etc.).
