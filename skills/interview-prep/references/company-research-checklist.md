# Company research checklist

What to gather, and where to look. Verify with at least one primary source per claim before it goes into the brief.

## Business basics

- **What they sell.** Read the product/pricing page. One paragraph: who buys this, why, how do they pay.
- **Stage and size.** Headcount (LinkedIn employees, last reported figure). Stage (private / public, Series A/B/C, profitable).
- **Funding history.** Crunchbase or news. Most recent round, amount, lead investor, date. What the round was for if disclosed.
- **Revenue / growth signals.** Public filings if listed. Press releases. Customer announcements. ARR if disclosed.
- **Competitors and how they position against them.** From the company's own messaging, then from one external comparison.

## Recent news (last 6 months)

- Product launches.
- Leadership changes.
- Layoffs / hiring freezes / reorgs.
- Regulatory / legal / security incidents.
- Major customer wins or losses.
- Strategic shifts (new market, sunsetting a product, repositioning).

Each item: date, one-sentence summary, source URL.

## Engineering / product stack (if relevant)

- Engineering blog — last 5 posts, what they discuss.
- Public GitHub org — primary repos, languages, recent activity.
- Job postings (other openings) — repeated stack mentions tell you the real toolchain.
- Tech conference talks by employees in the last 12 months.

## Team / role context

- The hiring team's structure if discoverable (LinkedIn "People" tab filtered by team/title).
- Recent hires into the same team in the last 6 months.
- Recent departures from the same team (LinkedIn shows this in profile updates).
- The reporting line for this role if the JD hints at it.

## Interviewer-specific

For each named interviewer:

- LinkedIn — current role, prior roles, tenure, what they posted recently.
- GitHub — recent activity, languages, projects.
- Twitter / Bluesky / Mastodon — only if public and recent (last 90 days). One-line summary of what they engage with.
- Conference talks / blog posts in the last 24 months — the topics they choose tell you what they'll probe on.

DO NOT include personal info (family, health, politics outside their professional commentary). Public professional output only.

## Hiring infrastructure

- **ATS** in use — derived from the apply URL pattern (see `../../../references/agency-and-ats-detection.md`). Tells you the application path the user already went through and may go through again for the final offer letter.
- **Hiring agency in the loop** — if the first round is an agency recruiter screen, name the agency, the recruiter's tenure there, and whether they specialize in this role family. LinkedIn shows this in their headline and prior placements.
- **Recruiter LinkedIn activity** in the last 30 days — what roles are they actively placing? That tells you what clients they're working with right now.

This section is short — 3–5 lines. It exists so the user knows the path-to-offer, not to over-research the agency itself.

## Culture signals

- Glassdoor — note the pattern, not individual reviews. Mention bias (very recent layoffs skew negative; recruiter campaigns skew positive).
- Blind threads if accessible.
- Employee LinkedIn posts in the last 90 days — what do current employees voluntarily talk about.

Mark every culture signal as "rumor / unverified". Treat as weak evidence, not fact.

## What to do with conflicting signals

If two sources contradict (e.g., a Glassdoor review claims layoffs but no press release exists), include both in the brief, mark the discrepancy, and let the user ask about it diplomatically in the interview ("I read mixed signals about Q3 — how is the team thinking about headcount going into next year?").

## Time budget

Cap the research at 25 minutes of fetches. Past that, you're hunting for noise.
